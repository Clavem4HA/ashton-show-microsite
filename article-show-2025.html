The 5 Questions We Answer Most at Every Dealer Show

Every year, we spend a few days at shows like SEIADA talking with dealers face to face. And every year, the same questions come up — not because dealers don't know their business, but because bond and insurance rules are genuinely confusing, state requirements change without much fanfare, and most of the resources available give you the statutory minimum without the context behind it.

Here are the five questions we hear most, with the answers we actually give.

1. "Does my credit score really affect my bond rate that much?"

Yes. It's the single biggest factor in your premium.

Dealer bonds are underwritten like credit products. A strong personal credit profile — typically 700 and above — can bring your annual premium down to 1% or less of the bond amount. A fair profile in the 600s often runs 2–4%. Below that, some carriers will decline outright.

On a $25,000 bond, that's the difference between paying $250 and $1,000 a year. On a $50,000 bond like Tennessee requires, the gap is even sharper. And it compounds over time — if you've been auto-renewing for years without checking, it's worth asking your agent to reprice it, especially if your credit has improved.

If your credit has improved since your last renewal, ask your agent to reprice. Don't assume the rate is fixed.

2. "Do I need a separate bond if I open a second location?"

It depends on the state — and that's not a dodge, it's genuinely the answer.

Mississippi has a blanket bond option: a single $100,000 bond can cover all your locations instead of a separate bond per lot. That can simplify renewals significantly when you're growing. But not every state works that way.

Alabama, Florida, and Tennessee generally tie bonds to each licensed location. Louisiana gets complicated for different reasons — they have two separate commissions depending on whether you're selling new or used vehicles, and district-based renewal schedules that vary by location.

The short answer: before you sign a lease on a second location, call us and tell us the state. The bond implications should be part of your expansion math from day one.

3. "When exactly does my bond need to renew?"

This is the one that bites dealers more than any other.

Alabama and Tennessee both have October 31 renewal deadlines. If you're an AL or TN dealer, that window goes faster than it looks when you factor in underwriting time, license coordination, and your own busy season heading into fall.

Florida's common expiration is April 30. Mississippi is December 31. Louisiana varies by district.

The mistake we see most often is treating the bond like a bill — just paying when the renewal notice shows up. That works until it doesn't. A claim, a credit change, or a carrier non-renewal can turn a 30-day window into a real problem. Start 90 days out, not 30.

4. "Can I just handle this online and skip the agent?"

You can. Some bonds in some states are genuinely that simple.

But here's where we see people get hurt: the online workflow gives you the statutory minimum. It doesn't ask whether your dealer type is classified correctly for your state. It doesn't flag that Louisiana's used-dealer commission is different from the new-dealer commission. It doesn't know that Tennessee's garage liability minimum has two conflicting figures in circulation.

It works fine until the ninth application. The tenth is where the gap shows up.

We're not protecting our business model — we're telling you what we actually see happen. The dealers who end up with a problem almost always had something that didn't fit the standard form.

5. "What's the difference between a dealer bond and garage liability?"

They're often required together, but they protect entirely different things.

A dealer surety bond protects the public — your customers, the state, and third parties — against fraud, unpaid taxes, or violations of dealer license law. If a claim is filed against your bond, the bonding company pays it and then comes back to you to recover. It's not insurance in the traditional sense; it's more like a credit instrument.

Garage liability insurance protects your dealership's operations — vehicles in your care, lot activities, customer interactions, test drives. It's what pays when something goes wrong on your end operationally.

Both are typically required for licensing, and both renew on a cycle. The coverage gaps that cause real problems usually sit at the edges where one policy ends and the other begins. Having them with the same agent helps.
